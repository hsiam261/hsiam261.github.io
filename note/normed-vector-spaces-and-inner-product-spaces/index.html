Normed Vector Spaces and Inner Product Spaces

Apr 03, 2025
note

Normed Vector Space

A normed vector space is a vector space $V$ paired with a function $\|\cdot\|: V \rightarrow \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ called the norm function, that assigns “lengths” to vectors. For $\|\cdot\|$ to be a valid norm it must satisfy the following conditions:

  • Non-negativity: $\|v\| \geq 0$ for all vectors $v$.
  • Non-degeneracy: $\|v\| = 0$ iff $v = 0$.
  • Absolute Homogeneity: $\|\alpha v\| = |\alpha|\cdot\|v\|$ for all vectors $v$ and scalars $\alpha$.
  • Triangle Inequality: For all pairs of vector $u$ and $v$ $\|u\| + \|v\| \geq \|u+v\|$.

Examples of Norms would be:

  • $L_p$-Norm $( 1\leq p < \infty)$: Is often expressed as $\|\cdot\|_p$ and is defined as $\|x\|_p = (\sum_{i=1}^n|x_i|^p)^{\frac{1}{p}}$. Clearly it is homogenous, non-degenerate and non-negative. It also satisfies the triangle inequality due to Minkowski inequality.
  • $L_{\infty}$-Norm: $\|x\|_{\infty} = \max_{i=1}^n|x_i|$.
  • $\|x\| = |f(x)|$ where $f(x)$ is a linear function of $x$ mapping to scalars.

Inner Product Space

An Inner Product Space is a vector space $V$ paired with a binary function $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle: V \times V \rightarrow \mathbb{F}$ called the inner product where $\mathbb{F}$ is the scalar field (i.e $\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$). For the function $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ to be an inner product it must have the following properties:

  • Left-Homogeneity: $\langle \alpha u, v\rangle = \alpha \langle u, v \rangle$ for all pairs of vectors $u,v$ and scalars $\alpha$.
  • Left-Additivity: $\langle u_1 + u_2, v\rangle = \langle u_1, v \rangle + \langle u_2, v \rangle$ for all vectors $u_1, u_2,v$.
  • Conjugate Symmetry: $\langle u, v\rangle = \overline{\langle v, u \rangle}$ for pairs of vectors $u,v$.
  • Non-negativity: $\langle u, u \rangle \geq 0$ for all vectors $u$.
  • Non-degeneracy: $\langle u, u \rangle = 0$ if and only if $u = 0$.

Note that inner product spaces only makes sense when we are defining vector spaces over ordered fields where the conjugate operation is well defined. For real fields, we just take the restriction of cojugation to reals (which is the identity function).

Choices For Inner Product Operators

Given any vector space $V$ we can have many choices of functions for the inner product operator. Two vectors are called orthogonal if their inner product is zero. Given an inner product operator and a basis of $V$, we can compute a orthogonal set of basis vectors (every pair of vectors in the basis is orthogonal) using something called the Gramâ€“Schmidt Process. A set of vectors are called orthonormal if each of the basis vectors have unit length (i.e has norm one) and are pairwise orthogonal. Gram-Schmidt Process gives us an orthonormal set of basis.

Theorem 1 (Gram-Schimdt Process): Every inner product space (in $\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$) of finite dimension1 has a orthonormal basis set.

Let $B=(v_1, v_2, ... v_n)$ be a basis of the vector space $v$ and let $x = \sum_{i=1}^n x_iv_i$ and $y = \sum_{i=1}^n y_iv_i$. Then,

$$ \begin{aligned} &\langle x, y \rangle \\ &= \langle \sum_{i=1}^n x_iv_i, \sum_{i=1}^n y_iv_i \rangle \\ &= \sum_{i=1}^n x_i \langle v_i, \sum_{i=1}^n y_iv_i \rangle \\ &= \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n x_i \overline{y_j} \langle v_i, v_j \rangle \\ &= y^*G_Bx \end{aligned} $$

where $G_B$ is the Gram Matrix for the basis set $B$. Gram matrix for a list of vectors $U = (u_1, u_2, ... u_n)$ is defined as

$$ G_U = \begin{bmatrix} \langle u_1, u_1 \rangle & \langle u_1, u_2 \rangle & \cdots & \langle u_1, u_n \rangle \\ \langle u_2, u_1 \rangle & \langle u_2, u_2 \rangle & \cdots & \langle u_2, u_n \rangle \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \langle u_n, u_1 \rangle & \langle u_n, u_2 \rangle & \cdots & \langle u_n, u_n \rangle \end{bmatrix} $$

If our basis set was orthonormal, we get the following:

$$ \begin{align} \langle x, y \rangle = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i \overline{y_i} \\ \end{align} $$

The inner product function that takes two vectors from $\mathbb{F}^n$ and computes $\sum_{i=1}^n x_i \overline{y_i}$ is known as the standard inner product.

Since all finite dimensional vectors can be represented by columns with respect to some orthonomal basis, all inner product operators for a vector space is actually the standard inner product function (when considering said representation).

Thus given a vector space, if we pick an arbitrary basis and call them orthonormal, the inner product operator becomes fixed and well defined. Since the same inner product operator gives rise to infinite number of orthonormal basis sets, fixing different basis sets as orthonormal do not always lead to different inner product functions.

Norms in Inner Product Space

The norm $\|\cdot\|$ of an inner product space $V$ is defined as $\|v\| = \sqrt{\langle v, v\rangle}$. $\sqrt{\langle v, v\rangle}$ already satisfy non-negativity and non-degeneracy conditions. Furthermore $\sqrt{\langle \alpha v, \alpha v\rangle} = \sqrt{\alpha \overline{\alpha} \langle v, v\rangle} = |\alpha| \sqrt{\langle v, v\rangle}$ i.e it satisfies the alsolute homogenity property. So to prove that $\sqrt{\langle v, v\rangle}$ is a norm function, all that is required is to show that $\sqrt{\langle v, v\rangle}$ satisfies triangle inequality.

First note that, $\langle u+v, u+v \rangle = \langle u, u \rangle + \langle v, v \rangle + 2\mathcal{R}\mathcal{e}(\langle u, v \rangle)$. Triangle inequality claims that $\langle u+v, u+v \rangle \leq \langle u, u \rangle + \langle v, v \rangle + 2\sqrt{\langle u, u \rangle \langle v, v \rangle}$. Thus, triangle inequality actually reduces to the following inequality:

$$ \begin{align} \mathcal{R}\mathcal{e}(\langle u, v \rangle) \leq \sqrt{\langle u, u \rangle \langle v, v \rangle} = \|u\|\|v\| \end{align} $$

We will infact prove a stronger result than Inequality 2 known as the Cauchy-Schwartz inequality. The Cauchy-Schwartz inequality is true for inner product spaces regardless of the field as we will prove here:

Theorem 2 (Cauchy Schwartz Inequality): Let $V$ be an inner product space and $u, v \in V$ be two vectors. Then $|\langle u, v\rangle| \leq \|u\|\|v\|$.

Proof: First note that $\langle x, 0 \rangle = 0$ for any $x \in V$. Thus, if $\|v\| = 0$ (i.e $v = 0$) then the cauchy-schwartz inequality trivially holds. Thus we only need to consider the case when $\|v\| \neq 0$.

Consider $\|u-tv\|^2$ where $t$ is a scalar. Due to non-negativity, $\|u-tv\|^2 \geq 0$ for any value of $t$. Now if we expand $\|u-tv\|^2$ (using additivity, homogenity and conjugate symmetry) we find that $\|u-tv\|^2 = \|u\|^2 + |t|^2\|v\|^2 - t\langle v, u \rangle - \overline{t}\langle u, v \rangle$. If we set $t = \frac{\langle u, v\rangle}{\|v\|^2}$, then we find $\|u-tv\|^2 = \|u\|^2 + \frac{|\langle u,v\rangle|^2}{v^2} -2\frac{|\langle u,v\rangle|^2}{\|v\|^2} = \|u\|^2 - \frac{|\langle u,v\rangle|^2}{\|v\|^2}$. Since $\|u-tv\|^2 \geq 0$, we have $\|u\|^2 \geq \frac{|\langle u,v\rangle|^2}{\|v\|^2} \implies \|u\|^2\|v\|^2 \geq |\langle u,v\rangle|^2 \implies \|u\|\|v\| \geq |\langle u,v\rangle|$.

$\quad \square$

Since Cauchy-Schwartz inequality is stronger than Ineqality 2, which is sufficient for triangle inequality, triangle inequality is also satisfied by the norm $\sqrt{\langle v, v\rangle}$. Thus $\|v\| = \sqrt{\langle v, v\rangle}$ is a valid norm.

Polarization Identities and The Relation Between Norm and Inner Product in an Inner Product Space

Given the inner product function in an inner product space, we can easily obtain the norm for that space by computing $\sqrt{\langle v,v \rangle}$. But instead of being presented with the inner product operator, what if instead we were only provided the norm? Can we express the inner product operator as an expression of the arguments using only vector addition, scalar multiplication, field operations for the scalar, and norm operator?

The answer is yes and is known as the Polarization Identities.

Theorem 3 (Polarization Identities): Let $x, y \in V$ be two vectors in an inner product space. Then,

$$ \langle x, y \rangle = \dfrac{1}{4}\sum_{\alpha \in \{1,-1\}} \alpha \|x+ \alpha y\|^2 $$

if $V$ is a vector space over the real field and

$$ \langle x, y \rangle = \dfrac{1}{4}\sum_{\alpha \in \{1,-1, i, -i\}} \alpha \|x+ \alpha y\|^2 $$

if $V$ is a vector space over the field of complex numbers.

The proof of this is just simplifying the expressions and left as an excercise for the reader.

Are All Norms Obtainable from Some Inner Product Operation?

We call a norm $\|\cdot\|$of a vector space $V$ is obtainable by inner product if there exists some inner product function $\langle \cdot , \cdot \rangle$ of $V$ such that $\|v\| = \sqrt{\langle v, v \rangle}$. Since, the inner product operation in inner product space is determined when we fix the norm operator, does this mean that all norms are obtainable by inner product? Are normed vector spaces and inner product spaces the same thing being represented differently?

The answer is no.

In an inner product space just the norm determines the inner product operator. But the when the space is not an inner product space, the polarization identities are not guaranteed. If the said norm was obtainable from some inner product function over the vector space, then the polarization identity uniquely identifies the said inner product function. But, if norm operation is not obtainable from any inner product function, the right hand side of the Polarization Identities will not have all the properties of a inner product function, namely we do not know if it is left additive or left homogenus. Note that conjugate symmetry will hold regardless. The polarization identities make it necessary for the inner product operation to be equal to either $\frac{1}{4}\sum_{\alpha \in \{1,-1\}} \alpha \|x+ \alpha y\|^2$ or $\frac{1}{4}\sum_{\alpha \in \{1,-1, i, -i\}} \alpha \|x+ \alpha y\|^2$ depending on the scalar field. But it is not sufficient and the inner product operation might not exist.

Not all norms are obtainable from some inner product function. In fact, only norms that are obtainable are the ones that are $L_2$ norms when the vectors are represented as column vectors with respect to some orthonormal basis since the only candidate for the inner product operation is then the standard inner product, which leads to the $L_2$ norm.

If we are representing vectors as column vectors with respect to some basis set that is not orthonormal, since an orthonormal basis set exists, we know there exists a matrix $A$ takes a column vector and changes its basis returns the column vector representing the same vector with respect to some orthonormal basis. At which point we can compute the norm of $x$ by computing the $L_2$ norm of $Ax$ which is just $\sqrt{x^*(A^*A)x}$. Note that $A^*A$ is a positive operator since $A$ is full rank and every positive operator can be represented as the multiplication of a full rank operator with its hermitian uniquely. If $M = D^*D$ is a positive definite operator, then $\sqrt{x^*Mx}$ represents the norm of $x$ obtained from the inner product operation which has the columns of $D^{-1}$ as an orthonormal basis set. Thus, the only norm functions obtainable from inner product spaces are of the form $\sqrt{x^*Mx}$ where $M$ is a positive definite matrix.

Jordan-Von-Neumann Theorem

The strategies in the previous section has some drawbacks. How exactly do you check if your the square of your norm is decomposible to $x^*Mx$ where $M$ is a positive definite matrix? And what if your vector space is not in the form of column vectors and in some abstract format?

A better neseccary and sufficient condition for a norm to be obtainable from an inner product space is given by Pascual Jordan and John Von Neumann in 1935.

Theorem 4 (Jordan Von Neuman Theorem): Let $V$ be a normed vector space with the norm $\|\cdot\|$. Then, the norm is obtainable from some inner product function in $V$ if and only if the norm satisfies the parallelogram property ie.

$$ \|x+y\|^2 + \|x-y\|^2 = 2(\|x\|^2 + \|y\|^2) $$

holds for all pairs of vectors $x, y \in V$.

Since norms obtained from inner product functions obey the parallelogram property, the only if part is very easy. The sufficiency part is not so much. The polarization identities leaves us with only one candidate for our inner product space and we have to show that it follows additivity and homogenity.

We will only show it for the complex case. The proof for the real case follows a similar procedure. To simplify our notation we define $\phi(x, y) = \frac{1}{4}\sum_{\alpha \in \{1,-1, i, -i\}} \alpha \|x+ \alpha y\|^2$. The norm will be obtainable from an inner product space if and only if $\phi$ is left additive and left homogenus.

Lemma 1: $\phi$ is left additive.

Proof: We will have to show the following:

$$ \begin{align} \phi(x + x', y) = \phi(x,y) + \phi(x', y) \end{align} $$

To prove Equation 3, we will break down to real and imaginary components and multiply both sides by $4$:

$$ \begin{align} & \|x+x'+y\|^2 - \|x+x'-y\|^2 = \|x+y\|^2 - \|x-y\|^2 + \|x'+y\|^2 - \|x'-y\|^2 \\ & \|x+x'+iy\|^2 - \|x+x'-iy\|^2 = \|x+iy\|^2 - \|x-iy\|^2 + \|x'+iy\|^2 - \|x'-iy\|^2 \\ \end{align} $$

Since if you replace $y$ with $iy$ in equation 4 it becomes identical to equation 5. Thus we only need to prove equation 4.

By applying the parallelogram law, we can write the following:

$$ \begin{align} & \|x+x'+y\|^2 + \|x-y\|^2 = 2\|x+\dfrac{x'}{2}\|^2 + 2\|\dfrac{x'}{2}+y\| \\ & \|x+x'-y\|^2 + \|x+y\|^2 = 2\|x+\dfrac{x'}{2}\|^2 + 2\|\dfrac{x'}{2}-y\| \\ \end{align} $$

Subtracting Equations 6 from Equation 7 we get the following:

$$ \begin{align} \|x+x'+y\|^2 - \|x+x'-y\|^2 - \|x+y\|^2 + \|x-y\|^2 = 2\|\dfrac{x'}{2}+y\| - 2\|\dfrac{x'}{2}-y\| \\ \end{align} $$

Again from the parallelogram law, we can write the following:

$$ \begin{align} & \|x'+ y\|^2 + \|y\|^2 = 2\|\dfrac{x'}{2}\ + y|^2 + 2\|\dfrac{x'}{2}\| \\ & \|x'- y\|^2 + \|y\|^2 = 2\|\dfrac{x'}{2}\ - y|^2 + 2\|\dfrac{x'}{2}\| \\ \end{align} $$

Subtracting Equations 9 from Equation 10 we get the following:

$$ \begin{align} & \|x'+ y\|^2 - \|x'- y\|^2 = 2\|\dfrac{x'}{2}\ + y|^2 - 2\|\dfrac{x'}{2}\ - y|^2 \\ \end{align} $$

If we subtract Equation 11 from Equation 8, we can derive Equation 4:

$$ \begin{align} & \|x+x'+y\|^2 - \|x+x'-y\|^2 - \|x'+ y\|^2 + \|x'- y\|^2 - \|x+y\|^2 + \|x-y\|^2 = 0 \nonumber \\ \implies & \|x+x'+y\|^2 - \|x+x'-y\|^2 = \|x'+ y\|^2 - \|x'- y\|^2 + \|x+y\|^2 - \|x-y\|^2 \nonumber \end{align} $$

$\quad \square$

We will now prove left homogenity of $\phi(x,y)$. First we will consider only the case where the scalar is a rational number. Subsequenty we will show that left homogenity holds even when the scalar is a real or complex number.

Lemma 2: $\phi(c x, y) = c\phi(x, y)$ when $c \in \mathbb{Q}$

Proof: We break the proof in to the following cases:

Case 1: $c = 0$. Then $\phi(c x, y) = \phi(0,y) = \frac{1}{4}\sum_{\alpha \in \{1,-1,i,-i\}}\alpha\|\alpha y\|^2 = \frac{1}{4}(\|y\|^2 - \|y\|^2 + i\|iy\|^2 - i\|iy\|^2) = 0 = 0 \times \phi(x,y)$.

Case 2: $c \in \mathbb{N}$. Due to Lemma 1, we know that $\phi$ is additive. Therefore $\phi(k x, y) = k\phi(x, y) \implies \phi((k+1) x, y) = \phi(k x, y) + \phi(x, y) = (k+1)\phi(x, y)$. Since, $\phi(c x, y) = c\phi(x,y)$ is true when $c=0$ (i.e case 1), it is true for all natural numbers.

Case 3: $c = -1$. $\phi(c x, y) = \phi(-x, y) = \frac{1}{4}(\|-x + y\|^2 - \|-x - y\|^2 + i\|-x + iy\|^2 - i\|-x - iy\|^2) = \frac{1}{4}(\|x - y\|^2 - \|x + y\|^2 + i\|x - iy\|^2 - i\|x + iy\|^2) = -\phi(x, y)$.

Case 4: $c \in \mathbb{Z}$. If $c$ is positive it is same as case 2. If $c$ is negative, we can then $c = -d$ for some positive integer $d$ and thus $\phi(d x, y) = d\phi(x,y)$. Using Case 3, we get $\phi(c x, y) = \phi(-d x, y) = -\phi(d x, y) = -d\phi(x,y) = c\phi(x,y)$.

Case 5: $c \in \mathbb{Q}$. If $c \in \mathbb{Z}$, then this case just reduces to case 4. If $c$ is not an integer but rational then there exists co-prime integers $p,q$ such that $q > 1$ and $c = \frac{p}{q}$. Then due to left additivity, we know that $q\phi(c x, y) = \phi(p x, y)$. Since $p$ is an integer, $\phi(p x, y) = p\phi(x, y)$ due to case 4. Thus $\phi(c x, y) = \frac{p}{q}\phi(x, y)$.

$\quad \square$

To prove that $\phi(c x, y) = c\phi(x,y)$ for real values of $c$ we will have to first establish the continuity of norm.

Lemma 3: Let $\|\cdot\|$ be a norm in some normed vector space $V$. Then $\|\cdot\|$ is continuous everywhere.

Proof: $\|\cdot\|$ is continuous at point $y$ iff $\lim_{x\to y}\|x\| = \|y\|$. We prove this with the epsilon delta defintion of limits.

$\lim_{x\to y}\|x\| = \|y\|$ if $\forall \epsilon > 0 \text{ } \exists \delta > 0 \text{ } \|x -y\| \leq \delta \implies |\|x\| - \|y\|| \leq \epsilon$. Now, due to triangle inequality, we know that, $|\|x\| - \|y\|| \leq \|x - y\|$. Thus, we can simply set $\delta = \epsilon$. This proves that $\lim_{x\to y}\|x\| = \|y\|$ i.e norms are continuous.

$\quad \square$

Now we can prove for the case of real values of $c$.

Lemma 4: $\phi(c x, y) = c\phi(x, y)$ when $c \in \mathbb{R}$

Proof: Any real $c$ can be expressed as the limit of a convergent sequence of rationals. Let $\{c_n\}_{n=1}^{\infty}$ be such a sequence. Let $f(\alpha) = \phi(\alpha x, y)$. Since, $\|\cdot\|$ is continuous $\alpha \rightarrow \alpha x$ is continuous, the square and addition are continuous functions, so is $f(\alpha)$. Since, $f(\alpha) = \phi(\alpha x, y)$ is continuous, $\phi(\lim_{\alpha\to c} x, y) = \lim_{\alpha\to c}\phi(\alpha x, y) = \lim_{n\to\infty}\phi(c_n x, y)$. Since, $c_n$ is rational, $\lim_{n \to \infty} \phi(c_n x, y) = \lim_{n\to \infty}c_n \times \lim_{n \to \infty}\phi(x, y) = c \times \phi(x, y)$.

$\quad \square$

We are finally ready to prove the left homogenity of $\phi$.

Lemma 5: $\phi(c x, y) = c\phi(x, y)$ when $c \in \mathbb{C}$

Proof: We prove it by breaking it into cases:

Case 1: Consider the case where $c=i$. Then $\phi(c x, y) = \phi(i x, y) = \frac{1}{4}(\|ix + y\|^2 - \|ix - y\|^2 + i\|ix + iy\|^2 - i\|ix - iy\|^2) = \frac{1}{4}(\|x - iy\|^2 - \|x + iy\|^2 + i\|x + y\|^2 - i\|x - y\|^2) = i\phi(x, y)$.

Case 2: $c=it$ where $t \in \mathbb{R}$. Then $\phi(c x, y) = \phi(i tx, y) = i\phi(tx, y) = it\phi(x, y) = c\phi(x, y)$.

Case 3: $c=s+it$ where $s,t \in \mathbb{R}$. We know that $\phi(c x, y) = \phi(sx + itx, y) = \phi(sx, y) + \phi(itx, y)$ due to additivity. But = $\phi(itx, y) = it\phi(x, y)$ and $\phi(sx, y) = s\phi(x,y)$. Thus, $\phi(c x, y) = c\phi(x,y)$

$\quad \square$

This concludes our proof of Jordan-Von-Neumann Theorem.
